Hi, I'm Uma.

I'm a Postdoctoral Research Fellow at Griffith University and a member of the Australian Research Centre for Human Evolution (ARCHE).

My research explores how DNA can help us understand human history. I work with both ancient and modern genomes to investigate population history, migration, genetic diversity, and evolution. Much of my work focuses on Aboriginal Australian genomics, where I use genomic data to better understand population relationships, ancestry, and the deep history of Australia.

What I enjoy most about genomics is that it brings together many different fields. My background spans genetics, bioinformatics, and computing, and I enjoy combining biological questions with computational approaches to uncover patterns hidden within large genomic datasets.

More recently, I have also become interested in studying deleterious mutations and mutation load across populations, and how genetic variation can influence both human evolution and health.

Outside of research, I genuinely enjoy teaching. Over the years, I have taught students from a wide range of backgrounds in both biological and computing disciplines.

Current Research

Ancient Aboriginal Australian Genomics

Understanding population history and genetic diversity using both ancient and modern Aboriginal Australian genomes.

Population History and Migration

Investigating how human populations are related to one another and how they have moved, mixed, and evolved over time.

Deleterious Mutations and Genetic Variation

Exploring how potentially harmful genetic variants are distributed across populations and what they can tell us about evolutionary history and disease susceptibility.

Bioinformatics and Genomic Data Science

Developing and applying computational methods to analyse large-scale genomic datasets generated through next-generation sequencing technologies.
